# Bookkeeping

Bookkeeping is the systematic process of recording, organizing, and maintaining the financial transactions of a business. It forms the foundation of the accounting system and ensures that every financial activity—such as sales, purchases, receipts, and payments—is documented accurately and consistently.

In a business, bookkeeping is typically performed on a daily basis. Each financial transaction is recorded in journals and later transferred to ledgers where transactions are categorized. This helps businesses maintain a chronological and organized record of their financial activity.

The main responsibilities involved in bookkeeping include:
- Recording daily financial transactions
- Maintaining the general ledger
- Managing accounts payable and accounts receivable
- Reconciling bank statements
- Preparing preliminary financial reports

Bookkeeping can be done using manual methods or accounting software. Modern businesses often use digital accounting systems that automatically track and categorize transactions.

There are two primary bookkeeping methods:
1. **Single-entry bookkeeping** – A simple method where each transaction is recorded once, commonly used by small businesses.
2. **Double-entry bookkeeping** – A more comprehensive system where every transaction affects two accounts (debit and credit). This method helps maintain the balance of financial records.

Accurate bookkeeping is important because it:
- Helps track business performance
- Ensures compliance with tax regulations
- Provides reliable financial data for decision-making
- Supports preparation of financial statements such as the balance sheet and profit and loss statement

Without proper bookkeeping, a business may struggle to understand its financial health and may face difficulties during audits or tax filings.
