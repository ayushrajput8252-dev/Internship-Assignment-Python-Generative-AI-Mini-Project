# Balance Sheet

A balance sheet is a financial statement that provides a snapshot of a company's financial position at a specific point in time. It shows what the company owns, what it owes, and the value invested by its owners.

The balance sheet is based on the fundamental accounting equation:

Assets = Liabilities + Equity

This equation ensures that the company's financial records remain balanced.

### Assets
Assets represent everything the company owns that has economic value. Assets can be categorized into:

- **Current Assets**: Assets expected to be converted into cash within one year, such as cash, accounts receivable, and inventory.
- **Non-Current Assets**: Long-term assets such as property, equipment, and machinery.

### Liabilities
Liabilities represent the company's obligations or debts that must be paid in the future.

Examples include:
- Loans
- Accounts payable
- Taxes payable
- Salaries payable

Liabilities can also be classified into:
- **Current liabilities** (due within one year)
- **Long-term liabilities** (due after one year)

### Equity
Equity represents the owner's share in the business after liabilities are deducted from assets. It includes the owner's investment and retained earnings.

Equity can increase through profits and decrease through losses or withdrawals.

The balance sheet is important because it helps stakeholders understand:
- The company's financial strength
- Its ability to meet short-term obligations
- The value of the business

Investors, managers, and financial analysts commonly use balance sheets to evaluate financial stability and make strategic decisions.
